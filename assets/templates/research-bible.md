# Research bible (nonfiction & self-help)

> For fiction, use `world.md` instead. This is the nonfiction foundation.

## Topic & scope
- **What this book covers.**
- **What it does NOT cover** (important — naming the boundary makes the book focused).

## Target reader
- **Concretely.** "Mid-career engineers moving into management," not "professionals."
- **What they already know** (so you don't over-explain).
- **What they want** (the job they're hiring this book to do).

## Core thesis
- **One sentence.** (Must match `thesis.md`.)
- The single claim the whole book argues.

## Key terminology
| Term | Definition | Preferred? | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| | | | |
- Terms to **avoid** (and what to use instead): list with reasons.

## The field's contested questions
- Where reasonable people disagree. The book must engage these, not pretend they don't exist.
- For each: what are the main positions, and who holds them?

## Steelman of the opposing view
- The strongest version of the argument against your thesis. (Mirrors `thesis.md`.)
- The book must engage THIS version, not a weak strawman.

## Sub-claims (3–7) that structure the book
1. ...
2. ...
- Each maps to a section of the argument arc.

## Existing literature
- **Comparable books** — what they got right, what they got wrong, what gap this book fills.
- **Foundational sources** — the works this book builds on (these go in `evidence.md`).
- **Where this book disagrees** with the consensus, and why.

## Currency & confidence
- How fast does this field move? (Affects how recent sources must be.)
- Where is the evidence strong vs. weak vs. emerging?
