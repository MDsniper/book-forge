# World bible (fiction)

> For nonfiction, use `research-bible.md` instead. This template is for fiction world-building.

Lore is weighted **40% of foundation score**. A weak world forces the writer to invent under pressure mid-chapter, which is where AI storytelling collapses.

## Geography
- **Places** (named, with sensory detail — sound, smell, light, material).
- **Distances & travel** (how long, how hard, what changes between regions).
- **Maps / spatial logic** (where things are relative to each other).

## History
- **Events that created present tensions** (history is not decoration — it explains why things are the way they are NOW).
- **What people remember vs. what really happened** (the gap is dramatic).
- **Recent vs. deep history** (recent drives plot; deep drives myth).

## Magic / technology / power system (if applicable)
- **Rules** — what it can do.
- **Costs & limitations** — what it can't do, what it costs to use. (Sanderson's 2nd Law: limitations > powers.)
- **Societal implications** — who has access, who's excluded, how it shapes politics/economics/religion. (This is where most worlds are thin.)

## Cultures & factions
- **Distinct groups** with their own values, aesthetics, and stakes.
- **What they want** and **what they fear**.
- **Conflicts between them** that exist independent of the protagonist.

## Economy & daily life
- What people eat, wear, do for work, do for fun.
- What's scarce. What's abundant.

## Cosmology / religion / belief
- What people believe about the world's origin and meaning.
- Where beliefs disagree.
- Where belief and reality diverge (the dramatic gap).

## Interconnection (the iceberg depth test)
- How does magic affect politics? How does geography shape the economy? How does history explain the present factions?
- A change in any one area should ripple into at least two others. If it doesn't, the world is a list, not a system.

## Iceberg depth
- For every fact on the page, there should be 3–5 implied beneath. The reader should sense more than they're told.
